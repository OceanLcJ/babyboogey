## Before You Upload: Match the Photo to the Dance

The AI has to infer movement that does not exist in the original picture. A clear source photo reduces that guesswork. The most useful question is not “Is this my cutest photo?” but “Can I clearly see the face and enough of the body for the movement I want?”

| Photo type | Best first dance | Why it is a safer match |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Face and shoulders | Gentle sway or small head-and-hand movement | Smaller motion asks the AI to invent less of the hidden body |
| Upper body visible | Playful bounce or arm-focused template | The shoulders and torso give the model more structure to animate |
| Full body visible | Bigger steps or a more energetic dance | Visible limbs make larger movement easier to estimate |
| Side profile or blocked face | Choose another photo first | Missing facial features often cause flicker or identity drift |
| Multiple people | Crop to one clear subject | Competing faces can make the animation attach motion to the wrong person |

A simple background is helpful, but it does not need to be perfectly blank. A bedroom wall, sofa, or uncluttered outdoor area can work. Problems are more likely when hands cross the face, a patterned blanket blends into clothing, strong filters remove facial detail, or the photo is a compressed screenshot.

## How to Make AI Baby Dancing Videos in 4 Steps

### Step 1: Pick the Right Photo

The input photo matters more than most people expect. Start with the original camera image when possible instead of a screenshot downloaded from a chat app. Look at the eyes, nose, mouth, shoulders, and hands at full size. If those details are already blurry in the still image, motion will not restore them.

**Best results**

- Face is clear (not blocked by hands, toys, or hats)
- Good lighting (avoid heavy shadows)
- Minimal blur (especially around eyes and mouth)
- Background isn’t too busy

**Avoid**

- Extreme angles (top-down or profile shots)
- Low-resolution screenshots
- Heavy beauty filters

If you have several similar photos, choose the clearest one rather than the funniest expression. You can add a more energetic template later, but a warped face is difficult to fix without changing the source photo.

### Step 3: Choose a Dance Style

Different dances fit different photos. The most dramatic template is not automatically the best one. Begin with a motion that fits the visible body area, then increase the energy only after you see that the face stays stable.

- **Cute / playful**: works great for smiling photos
- **Energetic**: better with full-body photos (if available)
- **Minimal motion**: a safer choice for close-up portraits

For a first attempt, gentle sways, small bounces, or hand-focused movement are usually more forgiving. Fast steps and large turns ask the model to invent more information outside the original frame. That can be fun, but it also raises the chance of distorted hands, stretched clothing, or an unstable background.

### Step 4: Generate, Preview, and Iterate

Generate the video and watch it once for enjoyment, then watch it again as a quality check. Look at the face during the largest movement, the outline around the hair and hands, and any straight lines in the background. Those areas reveal problems more clearly than the first frame.

- If the face looks “off”, try a clearer photo or choose a less aggressive motion style.
- If the background distracts, pick a cleaner photo next time.
- If the output is too fast, choose a calmer dance or shorter sequence (when available).

Change one thing at a time. If you replace the photo and the template together, you will not know which change improved the result. A good retry sequence is: keep the photo and lower the motion, then keep the gentler template and try a clearer photo only if the face still drifts.

## Troubleshooting Common Issues

### The face warps or flickers

**Fix**: Use a higher-quality photo, avoid heavy shadows, and try a lower-motion style.

Also check whether hair, a hand, a toy, or a hat covers part of the face. If the source is clear but the flicker begins during a fast turn, keep the photo and switch to a calmer template before spending another attempt on a different image.

### The body looks unnatural

**Fix**: Prefer photos where shoulders and torso are visible; avoid extreme close-ups.

Large dance motions need information about the body. When the original image ends at the neck, the AI has to invent everything below it. A shoulder-up crop is better paired with expressive head movement or a small sway than with footwork.

### The background gets messy

**Fix**: Use a photo with a clean background (plain wall, bed, etc.).

Busy wallpaper, striped bedding, crowds, and objects touching the subject can move or bend with the body. Cropping closer to one subject can help, as long as the crop still leaves enough room for the selected dance.

### The result no longer looks like the original photo

**Fix**: Return to an unfiltered, higher-resolution source and reduce the movement. Identity drift is more likely when the face is tiny, heavily filtered, partially hidden, or turned away from the camera.

### The first result is good but not worth unlocking

**Fix**: Keep the free preview and retry only when you can name a specific problem. Repeating the same photo-template pair without changing the input may spend credits without producing a meaningfully different result.
